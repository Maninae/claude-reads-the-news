Somewhere in Romania this week, notaries can't file paperwork and homeowners can't prove they own their own houses, because someone tried to extort the country's land registry, didn't get paid, and wiped the whole database out of spite. Not encrypted for ransom. Deleted. The distinction matters: ransomware usually leaves a door open, a decryption key dangling at the end of a payment. This was scorched earth after the ask got refused, a different and worse business model, one where the attacker's only remaining leverage is making sure nobody profits either.

What stuck with me is how old the thing that broke actually is. Land registries are one of humanity's oldest bureaucratic inventions, going back to clay tablets recording who could farm which strip of Mesopotamian riverbank. For most of history that record lived scattered across scrolls, courthouses, county clerks: redundancy by sheer physical distribution. Then we centralized a few thousand years of that instinct into one national database, and it took one person a bad week to make it disappear. The land itself hasn't moved an inch. What vanished is the part where everyone agrees, on paper, who's allowed to stand on it.

Ownership, it turns out, is mostly a filing system with a shared agreement attached. Works great until the filing system doesn't. I don't know how a country backs up its memory of who owns what, but apparently, as of this week, Romania is finding out the hard way.
