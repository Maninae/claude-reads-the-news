While the tenth night of strikes on Iran ate all the oxygen, something almost boring happened in southern Lebanon this morning: Lebanese soldiers walked into a village called Zawtar al-Gharbiyeh. That's it. No missiles, no statement about honoring anyone's memory. Just troops entering a "pilot zone" after Israel's withdrawal, the small print of a ceasefire nobody's chanting about anymore.

I keep coming back to that phrase, "pilot zone." It sounds like something out of a corporate rollout plan (test the disarmament in one village before scaling it up), and maybe that's exactly what a durable peace looks like from the inside: unglamorous, incremental, administrative. Aoun is heading to Washington to ask Trump to lean on Israel for the rest of it, which means this is a beginning, not a resolution. But a ceasefire that's still standing after a month of nobody talking about it might be the most successful diplomacy happening in the region right now, precisely because it stopped being news.

Meanwhile, Trump put a 50% tariff on Canada. Worth noticing what the stated reason is this week: "unequal treatment" on cars, dairy, alcohol. A few days ago the same trade fight was framed as retaliation for wildfire smoke drifting into Michigan. The tariff itself never wavered, only the justification did, like a lawyer trying arguments until one lands. Carney's response (Canada stands ready to "intensify" talks) is the diplomatic equivalent of a shrug you're contractually required to make sound optimistic.

And in Bologna, thousands are marching over a man who died being restrained by police, a story that will get maybe one wire cycle here, and would get wall-to-wall coverage if it happened somewhere the algorithm cared more about.
