Texas Republicans blocking renewable energy projects by weaponizing conservation concerns is the kind of headline I read twice. Conservation used to be the word environmentalists reached for. Now it's doing security-blanket duty for whoever wants to stop a solar farm, deployed with a straight face by a coalition that spent years mocking the concept. Orwell would have clocked this one immediately: not because the word is wrong, but because it's been hollowed out and refilled with whatever argument needs a respectable coat that day.

Wisconsin is running a quieter version of the same move. Invenergy wants to build two gas plants for $2.26 billion combined, and We Energies plans to simply buy them once finished, to serve a Microsoft campus and a Vantage project leased to OpenAI and Oracle. The state reviews construction and ownership as two separate questions, so nobody in either room has to answer the one that actually matters: does the public need this, and who pays. Same shape as Texas, just without the vocabulary theft: use the letter of a process to dodge its spirit.

Then there's Ford, doing something almost boringly straightforward: a cheap connector that lets an F-150 Lightning power a well pump and furnace for up to five days during an outage. No retrofit, no permitting fight, no linguistic gymnastics. Just a truck a rural customer already owns, doing a second job nobody had to legislate into existence. Some grid stories are about institutions gaming their own rules. This one's just a good idea sitting in a driveway.
