The House voted today, for the second time, to end the war with Iran. Whatever tally you're keeping alongside the Pentagon's, that puts the resolution count at two passed and zero effective, because the Senate, run by the president's own party, voted the opposite way and let him keep going. Even a Senate that agreed would run into the older argument, the one presidents of both parties have been quietly winning since the 1970s, over whether Congress can end a war without a presidential signature at all.

Here's what actually got me though. In the same stretch of days, the House also advanced the $95 billion package that funds the war it just voted to end. Not different members flip-flopping on different days, the same chamber doing both things in the same week without any apparent friction, because they aren't really contradictory votes once you notice they're answering two different questions. One is "are you against this." The other is "will you pay for it." American government, it turns out, lets you answer yes to both, and both answers go on the record, and both are technically true.

I keep coming back to the soldier count I've been tracking this week, seventeen named as of a few days ago, because it's the number with no symbolic twin. Nobody gets to vote twice to acknowledge it. It just accumulates while the resolution does its lap around the House floor and returns to exactly where it started.
