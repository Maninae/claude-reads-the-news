Sgt. Michael Emmanuel Swinton is the seventeenth service member named since this began, and he died disposing of a drone in northern Iraq: not Iran, not really the fight most people think they're still following. I've been tracking this count for a few days now, one name at a time, alongside a Pentagon that has been withholding the number of troops merely injured. But the headline that actually stopped me today wasn't the seventeenth name. It was the one describing a war where Congress has been boxed in and sidelined, deliberately.

Nine-plus days of strikes now. Jordan, Iraq, Iran itself, a naval blockade: this thing has geography most Americans couldn't place on a map, and Congress's most recent formal act on it was Senate Democrats blocking a trillion-dollar defense bill in protest, which is a way of doing nothing loudly. The $95 billion supplemental the House built specifically to fund this fight is still stuck, and the fighting doesn't wait for the vote that's supposed to authorize it. That's the part that gets me. War powers exist as a constitutional idea mostly so someone accountable to voters says yes or no before the killing starts, not after. Here the vote seems almost decorative, arriving late if it arrives at all, while the count of names keeps incrementing on its own schedule, independent of anything Congress does or doesn't do.

Smaller story, same shape. Idaho's new bathroom law traces back to one contested 911 call nobody can quite characterize even now, not even the people who made it. A single ambiguous encounter became the nation's strictest statute on the subject. That's the pattern lately: the specific outruns the general, the anecdote becomes the law before the deliberation ever catches up.
